Production

Event production refers to a professionally managed and well-coordinated process in which the arrangements and elements are brought together in order to deliver a functional and impressive event within the given time and budget frame. The following sections help you consider what needs to be booked and settled prior to the event. Things to take into consideration include e.g. choosing venue, coordinating the programme and speakers, staffing, catering, transport, accessibility, sustainability.

Venue

Suitable venue.

Identify the availability, accessibility, and size of the venue for the anticipated attendance and programme. Check how the facilities are equipped with technology, furniture, and restrooms. Always consider enough time for the setup, the event itself, and the disassembly and clean-up.

Room layout.

Room layout can be in theatre, classroom, rounds, cabaret, boardroom, u-shape. The layout can greatly effect the capacity of the room. It also takes a long time to change the layout. If the layout does need to be changed, build in extra time into your room booking. Outline the layout required when submitting your event notification to Estates + Facilities.

Event Notification

Prior to the event, the event organiser must always send the Estates + Facilities team a 'notification of facility use', assistance required and event security. Programme

Content Creation.

Consider what content adds value to the objective of the event and enhances the engagement of the audience. Consider various ways of presenting the content in a motivating format for your audience. Think about a suitable name for the event and its content with consideration to continuity and formalities. Use a consistent theme throughout the programme elements. Check how much of the content can be resourced from within IADT and how much needs to be booked from outside.

Speakers, hosts, facilitators.

Keynote speakers and hosts need to be approached well in advance. The owner of the event usually sends the invitation to the key speakers. Consider IADT community specialists to take the role of a host, chair, moderator, facilitator or MC. Give consideration to the diversity of any speakers, facilitators, panellists and similar positions that are being invited to your event. Questions to ask yourself can include:

  • Are they representative in terms of gender?
  • Are they representative in terms of race/ethnicity?
  • Are they representative in terms of age and/or career stage?
  • Are they representative in terms of disability?

Protocol.

Presentor guidelines

Speakers prep time.

Make sure you build-in time (15 minutes approximately) for speakers to meet with AV support staff, to set up microphones and run through how they work. If the event is being recorded, speakers will need briefing on camera positions and audio setup. How they interact with the technology will affect the end result.

Presentations.

If there are presentations, audio and oral materials, make sure you have the materials set up in advance, so they're uploaded, checked and ready to go. Request them from presenters in advance and save into folders and clearly label them for the AV team.
For events with a degree of complexity - more than just presentations - schedule a run through in the venue you've booked. If you're not sure if that applies to your event speak to the Events Team. IADT has limited capacity for AV support but will consider requests on a case-by-case basis. Consider having a laptop available where speakers can upload their presentations if they have not sent them in advance.

Speakers Photos & Bios.

When a presenter agrees, request a photo and bio which can be uploaded to the website and promotional information. Ensure that the speaker agrees to this.

Catering

When planning your event, think carefully about what food and/or drink you would like to give to your guests and where this will be served. It is important to set expectations before the guests arrive; in the additional information that you send out, let guests know if and when they will be given food or drink so that they know what to expect when they arrive.
You can also advise them of the facilities available on campus if you are not going to be providing refreshments. Where food is being provided, you should ensure that you give guests the opportunity to advise of any special dietary or allergy requirements.

Catering services.

Your first choice of catering should be campus catering, due to the health and safety concerns. Orders must be received 72 hours in advance. For larger events, contact as soon as possible. Remember to include dietary requirements in your order. Contact .
Catering order form

Catering orders.

To get a well-planned catering quotation, provide at least the following information: date and location, event and catering format (breakfast, lunch, dinner, cocktail, easy-going, formal, etc.), an estimated scheduled time for the serving, an estimated range of participants (min and max), budget range, food and drink preferences (e.g. vegan, vegetarian, gluten free, oat milk). When you place the order, ask when the caterer needs your final confirmation (number of participants, special diets) since this is important information for your production schedule.

Menu options and order quantities.

Favour domestic, local, seasonal, and organic ingredients and vegetarian menu. Always reserve water for guests, speakers and performers. If the event includes a dinner choose between a buffet or table service, as well as standing or seated service. If necessary, make arrangements for a tasting before confirming the order. If you have a backstage or break rooms for your hosts and performers, it is polite to reserve some snacks in the room.
Catering menu

Special diets.

Ask guests when registering or on invitation for special diets and food restrictions when registering. Ask the caterer to clearly mark the ingredients in the dishes and allergy information. Find out who in the catering staff can provide more information about the food ingredients.

Alcohol service.

It is not recommended to serve alcohol at IADT events. If, however, alcohol is served, remember to make sure all necessary licences are in order and the event organiser is aware of the restrictions of the license. Contact .
Alcohol statement

Food safety.

Any food you serve must be consumed at the specified time in order to comply with catering regulations, so ensure you build catering timings into the running order of your event.

Not providing catering.

If you are not going to be providing any catering or refreshments, you should advise attendees of the facilities available on campus.

Tableware and presentation.

Caterers usually provide tableware and napkins. When you make an order, pay attention to the quality of, and real need for, the products used. Try to avoid non-reusable products and disposable tableware.

Travel

Hotel planning.

If you add a hotel option for your guests to book and pay directly, remember to agree a quotation price with the hotel(s) beforehand. The deadline of the hotel quota(s) and the deadline for the event registration should always be the same. Try where possible to arrange for a booking code with the hotel for attendees to use when booking, rather than having to manage the accommodation yourself. for support if required.

Booking Hotels for Speakers.

If booking hotels for speakers, ensure that you are track the release dates for any bookings. Ensure the cancellation policy is in line with the event timeline of dates. For example, the speaker confirmation should be before the hotel cancellation date.

Visa assitance.

Should any of your speakers or attendees require a visa letter to attend the event, only provide this to them once they have paid for their registration.

Health & safety principles.

The organiser is always responsible for ensuring that the event is planned and implemented in such a way that people can participate safely, and so that the arrangements are functional even if something unexpected should happen. How to minimise risks and ensure safety of an event should be discussed well in advance. Number of participants, the time of the event, layout of the venue etc. all play a significant role in the safety of people.

Risk Assessments.

It is essential that a suitable risk assessment detailing the arrangements for reasonably foreseeable risk is created for your event. Your risk assessment should include contingencies to deal with incidents and situations as varied as contractors cancelling at short notice, severe weather, or the unavailability of key staff in your team. You will also need to consider the response to more serious emergencies, including major incidents that will require help from the emergency services and implementation of their emergency plans.
You should ensure appropriate management systems are in place for each phase of the event to make sure health and safety risks are controlled.
While the numbers onsite during the 'public' period will be significantly greater, the need for safety management during build up, load-in, breakdown and load-out is just as important as this is likely to be when the highest-risk work activities are carried out.
Sample Risk Assessment
All external contractors must compile separate risk assessments for their work, a method statement for the installation of any temporary structures and make available a copy of their third-party liability insurance.

First Aid

The level of First Aid provision you will need at your event will depend on the numbers attending, the profile of the audience and the type of event. This should be detailed in the Risk Assessment for your event. For smaller events, it may be sufficient to ensure some of the staff working at the event are trained in first aid but for larger events, it may be necessary to book First Aid provision from a recognised provider. A number of voluntary organisations will provide cover for a fee.
If an accident occurs during an event, you should take full details at the time of the incident, including the name and address of the injured person, the nature of the accident and where the accident occurred.
